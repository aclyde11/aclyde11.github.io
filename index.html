My primary technical research interests is currently virutal screening with deep learning. Here's a blurb on the other work I do:

AI is not emerging exclusively through economic power but also throughout the sciences as an emerging means of knowledge production. AI is becoming synonymous with the future of science. If AI's story in science looks as it did for technology companies, we ought to be deeply concerned about who maintains the ends of science, controls the means of science, and how the fruits of science are disseminated throughout society. SARS-CoV-2 is a timely highlight of the importance of science being an open, transparent, and communication-focused enterprise to share a common worldview for strategic and collective action planning among citizens. I aim to critically examine the natural sciences' adoption of artificial intelligence as a practice for knowledge production. As scientific research accelerates in conjunction with computing, we need to understand the implications of self-driving laboratories and ideas of autonomous discovery on the nuanced relationship between science, democracy, and citizens.

More technical work with drug discovery: Medicinal chemists and biologists use virtual screening to scan large chemical databases to find molecules of interest to perform expensive downstream analysis. The standard practice uses classical techniques such as structural docking or traditional machine learning to screen libraries on the order of 100k. The recent introduction of deep learning means we can score massive libraries (order tens of billions). And if that isn't enough, there have been recent successes in de-novo molecule generations. In my testing, these generators can output valid molecules at a rate of 25,000 unique to all known databases per second per GPU. In this regime, downstream analysis such as MD on even 0.001% would be intractable.

My work focuses on solving this problem in two ways:

  • Creating workflows and analysis that can intelligently sample these enormous libraries always to provide downstream analysis the best possible candidates
  • Rethinking the way lead discovery is performed, so we don't have to think of these steps as all unique. If we reframe the problem with the specific goal in mind, it may be the case the workflow arises different (postmodernism can teach you a bit about this concept)

In many ways, I am skeptical of typical supervised deep learning. The workflow of collecting data, generalizing over it, and predicting off it has its uses--but in the regime discussed, it seems aimless. And yes, I am very inspired by 20th-century French philosophy.
